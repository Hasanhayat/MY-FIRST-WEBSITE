SPECTRUM

Spectra are graphical representations that show how a physical quantity, such as intensity or number of particles, varies with another variable, often frequency or wavelength. Spectra are used across various scientific fields, particularly in physics, chemistry, and astronomy, to analyze the properties of matter and radiation. Below is a detailed note on spectra, including its types, generation, and applications.

Types of spectrum

  • Continuous Spectra:

    1. Defination:A continuous spectrum contains all wavelengths within a given range, without any gaps.
    2. Generation:Produced by incandescent solids, liquids, and densely compressed gases. For instance, a heated filament in a light bulb emits a continuous spectrum.
    3. Example:The visible light spectrum produced by a blackbody radiator.

  • Emission Spectra:

    1. Definition:An emission spectrum consists of bright lines or bands on a dark background, representing specific wavelengths emitted by atoms or molecules.
    2. Generation:Occurs when electrons in an atom or molecule fall from a higher energy level to a lower one, releasing energy in the form of light at specific wavelengths.
    3. Example:The bright lines seen in the spectrum of hydrogen gas.
